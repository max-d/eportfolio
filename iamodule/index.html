Introduction to Agent-Based Computing

During week one of the course, we were introduced to the concept of agent-based computation. Prior to this, I had encountered the term “agent” when observing Alpha Start AI playing StarCraft 2 against human opponents. However, at that time, I did not delve into the reason behind machines being referred to as “agents.” Wooldridge (2009) shed light on this term, defining an agent as a computer system existing within an environment, capable of perceiving it and autonomously taking actions. Despite this seemingly straightforward explanation, the theory turned out to be more complex and nuanced. It brought forth various questions, with “autonomy” being the primary but not the sole concept. Unlike a mere software program or daemon, which solely deals with inputs and outputs, an agent operates in a dynamic and volatile environment. Personally, this concept is new to me, yet it resonates well with my previous experience working with intricate distributed software systems. While my prior expertise was limited to traditional software approaches, I was pleasantly surprised to discover the widespread use of agent-based systems across multiple industries. Unfortunately, I was unable to participate in the initial collaborative discussion. Nonetheless, upon reviewing the posts later, I came across intriguing information about how diverse sectors embrace agent-based systems. For instance, I was fascinated to learn that researchers in biology employ “The Virtual Cell” systems to test hypotheses (vcell.org, 2023). However, when compared to conventional software solutions, agent-based systems are still in the developmental phase, with numerous research outweighing actual implemented solutions. The content covered in the first unit sparked numerous ideas regarding the implementation of agent-based solutions within the industry I currently work in.

First Order Logic

During the second week of our studies, we looked into First Order Logic (FOL), which serves as a means of knowledge representation. In the context of computers, the structuring of knowledge becomes essential for comprehension. My initial exposure to this concept occurred many years ago during my graduation when I undertook a Prolog programming course. Consequently, the concepts explained by Russell & Norvig (2021) felt quite familiar, harking back to my earlier learning experiences. However, despite this familiarity, I had never actively applied this knowledge, and initially, this unit appeared to be rather intricate.

The seminar conducted during the week significantly contributed to my understanding of First Order Logic, clarifying its concepts and principles. As a software engineer, much of my work revolves around bridging human-understandable industry knowledge and processes with machine-readable code. Consequently, grasping the fundamentals of First Order Logic was relatively easy. Nonetheless, limited experience in declarative programming could present hurdles to understanding FOL. Most programming languages predominantly adopt an imperative approach to organising program flow. Consequently, I encountered some difficulties initially, but I soon realised that FOL can be pretty straightforward and practical for complex systems.

Hybrid Agent Architectures

During the fourth week, we delved into Hybrid agent architectures. This particular architecture combines both proactive and reactive approaches in the design of agents, making it potentially valuable for tackling complex problems. Each approach, whether reactive or proactive, has its own strengths and weaknesses, and its effectiveness depends on the specific task at hand. However, the hybrid architecture offers a more flexible and adaptable approach. I found Wooldridge’s (2009) theoretical explanations and practical implementations, such as “Stanley,” to be particularly insightful. The multi-layer approach, with its various directions of interactions, initially puzzled me, but I eventually grasped its significance. One aspect that still raises some doubts for me is the practical implementation of these architectures. Based on my commercial experience, I have come to believe that “good” solutions tend to be the simplest ones, which can be developed and maintained by less experienced developers, with a focus on resolving straightforward problems. This approach has proven useful throughout my decade of working with distributed systems, designing and managing them. I don’t oppose sophisticated solutions that can efficiently solve complex problems, especially in the realm of AI. However, in the modern world, with its vast computational resources and cloud technologies, there is a push towards designing solutions that are both simple, possibly ineffective and resource-intensive. ChatGPT itself is an example of such a resource-expensive solution. This could be an interesting topic for further discussion and exploration.

Working Together

In week six, we focused on the practical aspect of KQML. Unsurprisingly, it appeared to have a declarative LISP style, providing solid opportunities to structure messages. However, I have found it too high level, without practical meaning to what we call modern systems. KQML provides a simple model for understanding agent communication, but I can’t imagine actual distributed systems which will gain some advantages from implementing ACL as a high-level protocol description. It rather fits classic multi-agent systems, which are built around meaningful knowledge exchange.

During another part of the week, we spent our time completing our assignment. Early on, we faced the challenge of dealing with different time zones among group members. However, despite the initial struggles, we managed to find a way to achieve at least one hour of daily group communication later on.

Instead of adopting a sophisticated Software Development Life Cycle (SDLC) with its potential overheads, we opted for a straightforward and simple workflow. Each team member focused on their respective strengths, allowing us to leverage everyone’s expertise effectively. Additionally, every participant was encouraged to share their ideas regarding the multi-agent system architecture based on their understanding of the subject.

Ultimately, we collectively decided to concentrate on a single-agent architecture, which could serve as a foundation for building a multi-agent system in the future. Working together as a team has been a rewarding experience, and we are looking forward to exploring the possibilities further.

We decided to use a simple Agent design consisting of 3 parts: sensors, an analysis module, actuator. All agents should have the same basic class, which implements abstract interaction between these three modules. Particular agent realisation should have a basic agent class and custom implementation for each module. This later could be used to implement a dependency injection pattern, which allows flexible implementation of the system, when different specialists could focus on developing functionality, they are experienced at. I see this approach as a good choice for systems which demand effective collaboration of AI specialists and experienced software engineers.

For our first version, we created a simple playground which consisted of one Agent whose responsibility was to read the environment, get DNA sample requests, match them to the database and create a notification if the sample was found. Nevertheless, the design was simple, and we spent some time to make it work properly. A test playground was created to demonstrate that our model worked properly.

Personally, I enjoyed this assignment because it is what I like about software engineering: designing and building PoCs. My teammates were also excited we got such good results. Both the lecturecast and reading list provided sufficient detail to understand the basics. However, before the course, I thought NLP was a less complex subject. However, it also revealed many complex ethical and philosophical problems, which are somehow inevitable when we work with phrases, meanings and interpretations. I enjoyed to deep down into the technical aspects of NLP but was puzzled by some state of frustration when it came to deeper analysis of language structure.

Also, this week we finished our collaborative discussions regarding agents’ communication in multi-agent systems.

Multi-agent systems have become increasingly prevalent in various domains, ranging from robotics and automation to social networks and smart cities. As the complexity of these systems grows, the need for effective communication and coordination among agents becomes crucial. Agent Communication Language (ACL) is a powerful tool that facilitates interaction and collaboration among agents, enabling them to work together towards a common goal. ACL could be considered a high-level language which provides suitable tools for transferring knowledge in multi-agent systems (Finin et al. 1993). All advantages and disadvantages of ACLs are mainly related to the application area and system size. For small multi-agent systems, ACLs may be more profitable. Complex multi-agent systems could have a massive array of data with complicated structures and agents which works with different ontologies. The wider the distribution of the multi-agent system, the more complicated messaging among agents could become. The difference in knowledge that agents operate may require significant efforts to align ontologies, which may cause further complexity raise. Large systems could potentially have the same problems that any extensive distribution system has, such as connection problems, queue management complexity, data fragmentation and accessibility (Twardowski et al., 2014).

However, researchers already provide experimental frameworks which allow the implementation of ACLs solution atop of systems, which resolve mentioned problems. For example, Multi-Agent Framework (Kawamura et al. 2005) allows implementing agents’ communication in multi-agent systems using XML-RPC for messaging. Furthermore, the Domain-Driven Design approach may help with dividing such systems into layers, where each layer represents a realisation of different elements (Steinegger, 2017). For example, ACL may be isolated on the top domain level, while protocol communication, data representation, and parallel computations could be done by low-level code. Such an approach could decrease the potential complexity of the Agent’s implementation for knowledge domain specialists while providing flexibility and performance for agent communication.

Overall, I enjoyed the discussions and was impressed by how modern technologies empower agent-based systems.

Deep Learning in Action

During week 10, our focus shifted to the practical aspects of deep learning applications. While AI still can’t fully compete with humans in certain domains, machine learning algorithms have the potential to surpass human limitations in specific tasks. For instance, though ML models may not yet achieve the same precision as humans in object recognition, they excel in recognising objects faster and over extended periods with less effort. Industries that don’t necessarily require absolute precision can benefit from using deep learning models, as they offer profitable solutions. It’s important to note that AI is not meant to replace humans but rather to empower them. By taking on tasks that demand rapid context changes, making multiple choices, or handling repetitive actions, AI can complement human abilities. For example, projects like Gitlab Copilot cannot replace software engineers, but they can certainly enhance a developer’s performance by providing useful assistance. Similarly, while ChatGPT may not solve complex problems outright for students, it offers structured information that can expedite the process of developing comprehensive solutions. In my opinion, supporting deep learning research and solutions is a positive step forward, as it equips us better to tackle future challenges. Embracing AI technology wisely can lead to a more efficient and productive world where humanity will be prepared for future challenges much better.

In Unit 12, we explored the future of agent-based deep learning technologies. I’m hopeful that this field will progress at an accelerated pace.
